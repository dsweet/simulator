# Economics Persona Design Brief

## Overview

Six fictional economics graduates, each representing a distinct path an economics degree can become. Two pursue meaningful careers directly after graduation; four go on to PhD programs in varied fields. All start from the same IB/AP baseline. Course plans use the UPS curriculum (semester-based, 4 courses per term). At UPS, HL IB exams (min 5) grant credit — the student enters with credit for MATH170 (Calculus I via IB Math A&A HL), MATH160 (Statistics via AP Stats), and a few gen-ed units from IB Lit HL and History HL.

## Constraints

- 4 of 6 pursue PhD programs — varied fields, varied institutions, varied types of impact
- 2 have non-academic outcomes with genuine societal impact
- All driven by intellectual curiosity about economic systems and human behavior, not prestige or earning potential
- Every course in every plan comes from the existing UPS Economics course catalog
- Prerequisite chains are respected
- Senior thesis (ECON 399) required for all

## The 6 Personas

### 1. The Community Builder — Rosa Gutiérrez-Park
**Brief:** Grew up in a small farming community in the Yakima Valley watching her parents — a Mexican-born orchard manager and a Korean-American agronomist — navigate the economics of small-scale agriculture: unpredictable crop prices, predatory lending, and a banking system that didn't speak their language. Takes development econ, public finance, behavioral econ, and urban econ alongside sociology and community-focused electives. Becomes a community development loan officer at a CDFI, eventually directing a regional economic development nonprofit connecting rural communities with capital and technical assistance. Non-PhD.

### 2. The Data Storyteller — Marcus Obi-Chen
**Brief:** A newspaper kid from a Chicago suburb who ran the school paper and noticed that economic statistics in news stories were either too dry for readers or too simplified for accuracy — the truth always fell through the gap. Stacks econometrics and applied statistics with creative writing, graphic design, and data science. Becomes an economics reporter, then a data visualization journalist at a major outlet, then founds a fellowship program training underrepresented journalists to cover economic policy with quantitative rigor and narrative clarity. Non-PhD.

### 3. The Ecosystem Economist — Linnea Johansson
**Brief:** Grew up on San Juan Island watching salmon populations decline while waterfront development boomed. Her parents — a marine biologist and a fishing boat captain — talked about the tension between economic growth and ecological health every night at dinner. Stacks environmental economics with biology, philosophy of science, and probability theory. PhD at UC Berkeley in environmental and resource economics, designing market mechanisms for ecosystem services that reflect ecological complexity rather than reducing nature to a price tag.

### 4. The Microfinance Researcher — Amir Okoye-Sharma
**Brief:** Son of a Nigerian immigrant doctor and an Indian immigrant engineer, spent a summer before college in rural southeastern Nigeria visiting his grandmother and seeing how microfinance programs actually worked on the ground — some transformative, some extractive, most somewhere in between. Takes development economics, behavioral economics, and deep econometrics. PhD at MIT in development economics, running randomized field experiments on financial inclusion programs across West Africa to figure out which interventions actually help and which just look good in grant proposals.

### 5. The Education Economist — Jade Whitehawk
**Brief:** Grew up on the Tulalip reservation north of Seattle and attended schools where per-pupil spending was a fraction of the neighboring Mukilteo district's budget. Watched talented teachers leave every year because the funding wasn't there. Takes economics of education, labor economics, and applied statistics alongside Indigenous studies. PhD at Harvard in education economics, studying the causal effects of culturally responsive curricula and equitable funding formulas on educational outcomes in tribal communities — research designed not to extract knowledge from her community but to serve it.

### 6. The Choice Architect — Theo Papadopoulos
**Brief:** A chess player and amateur poker theorist from suburban Seattle who noticed that his opponents made predictable irrational decisions under time pressure — and wondered whether the same patterns shaped how people made high-stakes choices about health insurance, retirement savings, and medical treatment. Stacks game theory, behavioral economics, and psychology of decision-making with philosophy (logic, philosophy of mind). PhD at the University of Chicago in behavioral economics, studying how the design of information environments shapes health-related decisions — whether the way Medicare plans are presented systematically leads people to choose against their own interests.

## How They Differ

| Dimension | Variations |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Outcome** | 2 industry/nonprofit · 4 PhD |
| **PhD fields** | Environmental econ · Development econ · Education econ · Behavioral econ |
| **PhD institutions** | UC Berkeley · MIT · Harvard · Chicago |
| **Non-PhD paths** | Community development finance · Data journalism |
| **Sophomore exploration** | Biology/environment · Data/writing · Statistics/math · Development/culture · Education/Indigenous studies · Psychology/philosophy |
| **Econ electives** | Development + Public Finance + Urban (Rosa) · Econometrics + Game Theory + Financial (Marcus) · Environmental + Game Theory + Public Finance (Linnea) · Development + Behavioral + Inequality (Amir) · Education + Labor + Behavioral (Jade) · Game Theory + Behavioral + Law & Econ (Theo) |
| **Diversity course** | AAS 200, HISP 200, GWSS 201, AFAM 200, AAS 300 |
| **Arts course** | Ceramics (most) · Graphic Design · Digital Photography |
